Projects

Energy-Efficient Mining Truck Challenge – Wabtec Corporation

As part of a course at Penn State University, I was tasked with developing a sustainable model for Wabtec mining trucks that reduced their dependency on fossil fuels while incorporating innovative energy solutions. I named this project CATBAT, emphasizing the integration of catenary wires and battery power for sustainable operations.

Through extensive research and consultations with professors and assistant professors specializing in energy systems, I evaluated various renewable energy sources and narrowed the options down to piezoelectricity and biofuels. Ultimately, I chose piezoelectricity for its innovative, sustainable, and self-sufficient characteristics.

For this project, I designed a system that incorporated piezoelectric plates into the massive tires of the mining truck. These plates harnessed the pressure generated within the tires during operation to produce electricity, which was stored in onboard lithium-ion batteries. This system provided reliable energy capable of powering the truck at speeds of up to 40 km/h while serving as a backup for off-grid operations.

To further enhance sustainability, the CATBAT system integrated a flexible pantograph arm connected to overhead catenary wires. This extendable arm allowed for efficient energy transfer from external sources to the truck, ensuring continuous operation while reducing reliance on fossil fuels. To support the system, I proposed telescopic support poles to suspend the wires at a height of approximately 30 feet, enabling quick installation and scalability for various mining sites.

The combination of piezoelectricity, lithium-ion batteries, and the catenary system significantly reduced the truck's environmental impact and operational costs. Using SolidWorks, I modeled the truck components and systems, ensuring mechanical stability and feasibility. MATLAB simulations were employed to analyze energy efficiency, system durability, and stress distributions, validating the design's practicality.

This innovative project was recognized for its potential to revolutionize the sustainability of heavy industrial vehicles. I was honored to present CATBAT to the entire engineering department and Wabtec Corporation officials, receiving praise for its creativity, feasibility, and transformative impact on mining operations.

Research on the Aerodynamics of Formula 1 Cars

My passion for Formula 1 and its challenges in ensuring safer racing inspired me to embark on this project during high school. The project focused on addressing the issue of turbulent wake produced by Formula 1 cars as they cut through the air at high speeds. This wake disrupts the trailing car's airflow, causing instability in steering and increasing the risk of accidents, particularly on high-speed straights. With regulations encouraging closer racing, turbulent wake became a significant safety concern.

Determined to find a solution, I focused on redesigning the aerodynamics of Formula 1 cars to create a wake with a smoother, more laminar flow, minimizing disruptions for trailing cars. Using SolidWorks, I performed simulations to analyze and optimize airflow starting at the front wing, which plays a crucial role in determining the flow over the entire car. The initial flow generated by the front wing heavily influenced how air separated at the rear of the car, which directly impacted the wake's turbulence. By modifying the front wing geometry, I aimed to control airflow from the onset, ensuring smoother transitions and reducing turbulence at the car’s rear.

As part of my research, I studied simulation data from previous generations of Formula 1 cars and observed a progressive decrease in turbulent wake over the years. This analysis revealed methods used by engineers to mitigate turbulence, such as the elimination of the Y250 vortex. By eliminating this vortex in my design, I reduced accelerated airflow through the front wing, which contributed to smoother airflow around the car. I adopted a similar method but employed a more aggressive approach to achieve a fully laminar flow at the car’s rear.

In addition to eliminating the Y250 vortex, I incorporated active aerodynamics into my design. Active aerodynamics involve adjustable components such as movable flaps and winglets that dynamically respond to driving conditions. These components optimized the car's aerodynamic profile by adjusting airflow patterns in real time. For example, I designed movable rear wing elements that could adjust their angle of attack to direct airflow more precisely, minimizing turbulence at high speeds while maintaining downforce for cornering stability.

The combination of these strategies—eliminating the Y250 vortex, employing active aerodynamic components, and optimizing airflow from the front wing—allowed me to significantly reduce turbulent wake while enhancing overall vehicle stability. Using MATLAB, I also modeled stress distributions on key components to ensure they could withstand intense forces at high speeds.

The findings from this project demonstrated the feasibility of reducing wake turbulence to achieve safer and more competitive racing. My simulations confirmed that my design achieved a smoother, more laminar airflow, benefiting trailing cars. The results of my research were published on ResearchGate, where they were recognized for their innovation and potential application in motorsport engineering.
